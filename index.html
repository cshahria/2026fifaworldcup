Recovery Day — Three Matches, Two Friends, One Paraguay Sighting

A day off between shifts, spent watching football the right way. Germany kicked things off against Curaçao — it was tight at 1–0 for a bit, then Germany took over and ran away with it. The Netherlands vs. Japan match that followed was the more tense affair. In the afternoon, friends joined for Ivory Coast vs. Ecuador — a pleasant game with a nice breeze, Ivory Coast winning. Long day that started at 10:00 AM.

During lunch I spotted the Paraguay national team returning to their hotel, full police escort. The routine is settling in: 9 miles each way on the bike, 45 minutes, solid conversations at the square. Already planning to come back with the group for Colombia vs. Portugal on June 27.

First Shift — Fan ID Crew, Gate B, Switzerland vs. Qatar

The alarm went off well before dawn. I was out the door around 5:00 AM, on my bike, and at the stadium by 6:00 — entering through the back via the San Tomas Aquino Creek Trail to SF Bay Trail. Security waved me through without issue, though I briefly went in the wrong direction and circled back. The ride itself was quieter than expected, with one notable exception: a police action right at the corner of Saratoga Avenue and Williams, where officers had smashed a car window and pulled someone out. Other than that, the streets were empty at that hour — just me and a stillness that wouldn't last long.

Once at the stadium, I met Linda near the fan launch area — she's retired from EMC and now working with the hospitality team. I also reconnected with several teammates from Fan Operations. We assembled as a group, ran through check-in, and at 8:10 AM headed out to our station: Gate B, the VIP line entrance, just as security checks were opening for the day.

Our assignment was Fan ID distribution. Boxes of RFID-embedded cards were waiting for us, exactly as covered in the virtual training sessions. The three of us on the crew set up our table and started handing them out. The opening was quiet — gates had opened on schedule at 9:00, giving us about an hour to organize — but then the surges started. Fans arrived in waves, and the three of us worked steadily to keep the flow moving. The most common question by far: "What's that long line going to the Fan Zone?" The answer was mascots, drums, and an atmosphere building by the minute.

The Fan ID itself is an NFC-enabled card that fans tap on their iPhone or Android to unlock a virtual experience throughout the tournament — match information, interactive content, and upcoming giveaways. Explaining the concept quickly and clearly to hundreds of different people across language barriers is its own kind of skill. The most physically demanding part of the whole shift, though, was untangling the Fan ID lanyards — they come bundled and knotted, and getting them sorted fast while the line builds is genuinely tricky work.

As for the match itself — even before gates fully opened, we caught word that Switzerland had scored off a penalty, and by the time we wrapped distribution and made our way to the crew cafeteria for lunch, Qatar had equalized. We watched the second half from the staff cafeteria — chicken, rice, broccoli, and salad — and caught up with Alex from Walnut Creek and Efren from San Jose, trading notes about the USA's 4–2–1 win against Paraguay the night before. It was exactly the kind of crew energy that makes a long day feel good.

After lunch we walked the concourse, grabbed some photos with Spencer and Jose from the Fan Operations leadership team, and checked in with F. Mendes before the shift wrapped. The final result — Switzerland 1, Qatar 1 — kept things tight. Crowd was roughly 60–70% Qatar supporters, with strong and enthusiastic representation throughout. All in all: a clean first shift, a well-run operation, and a meaningful first look at what this tournament feels like from the inside. Next up: Monday, June 16.

Opening Kickoff — The World Cup Has Arrived

The tournament is officially underway. I arrived at San Pedro Square Market around 10:00 — the opening ceremony was already streaming and the energy was building fast. Early on I took the opportunity to reconnect with a friend from Frankfurt — a quick check-in from a fellow football fan. Then came the Mexico fans. A lot of Mexico fans. The atmosphere at San Pedro Square was vibrant in the best possible way — it felt like the city actually showed up.

It was hot. 35°C — that's 95°F — and barely a cloud in the sky. No shade-seeking here, just full sun and the kind of heat that makes cold drinks disappear fast. Nobody seemed to mind.

The crowd wasn't just fans either. Reporters were everywhere including Swiss TV (I think). Mayor Matt Mahan was there, I shook his hand while he was walking with Zaileen "Z" Janmohamed, the Bay Area Host Committee CEO, to the stage. News anchors, cameras, official-looking lanyards. There was a real sense that this was a civic moment, not just a watch party.

The match result — a 2–0 victory — drew some pointed commentary from the German press. But from where I was standing, surrounded by supporters who've been waiting years for this, the final score felt almost secondary. This was a celebration. The fans were happy. The World Cup is here.

Day 2
Sunday, June 7, 2026 📍 San Francisco Bay Area Stadium, Santa Clara, CA

Fan Operations Training — Inside the Venue, Less Than a Week to Go

I arrived well before the 0930 scheduled start — early enough to mingle before the room filled up. One of the first people I met works in project management in the financial sector, lives nearby, and is also thinking about biking to the same light rail station to commute to the stadium for the matches. A good reminder of how wide this volunteer network runs — fan operations, media, catering, broadcasting, all converging in the same building.

The training opened with some striking numbers: over a million people applied to volunteer for FIFA World Cup 2026. In the Bay Area alone, roughly 1,000 were selected. The team lead, who has been here building this out for a full year, set the tone simply: stay positive, work as a team. It's a large, layered operation with FIFA staff, multiple functional areas, and thousands of additional stadium personnel.

The day covered a lot of ground — security awareness, the fan rulebook, Rules of Play, and the two shift structures. The fan operation center will be open before shifts and we're welcome to stay at the volunteer center after, but accreditation passes are strictly for shift time only. The team was clear on that.

One important note for June 13th: it's expected to be in the high 80s °F / 30 °D. The team stressed this repeatedly — wear the hat, apply sunscreen, drink water. Hydration breaks and a 45-minute meal break are built into every shift. The Clipper cards for public transit will activate on each match day.

The group today came from everywhere in the Bay and across the US. Someone in the room had volunteered at the 1998 World Cup in France. Volunteers who drove hours or took long train rides just to be here. About 40–60 people in fan operations alone. The venue was still being set up around us — which somehow made it feel even more real. Six days to go.

🎥 Gear haul — June 1

Accreditation Day — It's Official. I'm In.

It started with a drive up to the Santa Clara Convention Center and a parking spot on the upper deck — ordinary enough. But the moment I walked toward the main distribution center, something shifted. This was no longer just anticipation. This was real.

The check-in process felt exactly like airport security: passport out, scan, verify. There's something about that ritual — the small formal moment where a system confirms who you are — that makes the whole thing feel weighty. Two fellow volunteers guided me through with calm efficiency. Photography wasn't permitted in the accreditation hall, and I respected that completely. Some moments you hold in your head instead.

Then the doors opened to the uniform center. Trying on shirts in different sizes, finding the right pants, lacing up the Adidas shoes. It sounds mundane but it isn't — every item is a piece of something larger. I walked out with a full bag: four t-shirts, one long pant, one short, Adidas shoes, accreditation pass, transportation pass, and additional gear for the six games ahead.

I also met one fellow volunteer from the Fan Operations team. There are 87 of us. We'll be guiding fans through San Francisco Bay Area Stadium Stadium — wayfinding, seat assistance, announcements, keeping the energy good. I don't know my exact position yet, but training starts June 7th and the first real game — Switzerland vs. Qatar — kicks off June 13th. Shifts start well before kickoff., which means early mornings and long days. I can't wait.

Today felt like the starting gun. The gear is packed. The pass is real. Six games between now and July 1st.
